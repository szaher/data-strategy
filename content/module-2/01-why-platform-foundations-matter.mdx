## Overview

In Module 1, you designed a data strategy, assessed organizational maturity, evaluated reference architectures (managed-cloud-first, K8s-centric, hybrid), and built a roadmap. You made decisions about ownership models and picked technology patterns. But here's the uncomfortable truth: **strategy without platform foundations is just PowerPoint**.

The platform is the concrete reality beneath your strategy. It's the substrate that determines whether domain teams can ship weekly or quarterly, whether new data sources take hours or months to integrate, and whether your costs are predictable or spiral out of control.

This lesson reframes the platform as a **product** — not infrastructure that "just works" in the background, but a deliberate offering with customers (domain teams), an interface (golden paths and APIs), and a developer experience. You'll learn what platform teams actually provide, the distinction between golden paths and guardrails, and how to bridge Module 1's ownership models into concrete platform interfaces.

By the end of this lesson, you'll understand why platform work isn't optional overhead but the difference between a strategy that executes and one that stalls.

---

## The Problem: Ticket-Driven Ops Teams

Most organizations start with an infrastructure or data engineering team that operates as a **ticket-driven ops team**. Domain teams file tickets: "I need a Kafka topic," "Can you provision a database?" "My pipeline needs more memory." The platform team processes these tickets one by one — manually provisioning resources, writing custom configs, debugging one-off issues.

This model has a fatal flaw: **it doesn't scale**. Every new domain team, every new data source, every new pipeline becomes a bottleneck. The platform team becomes the constraint. Lead times stretch from days to weeks to months. Domain teams wait, frustrated. The platform team burns out, buried in toil.

> **The symptom:** If your data platform team's backlog is measured in JIRA tickets instead of product features, you're running an ops team, not a platform team.

Ticket-driven ops teams optimize for **keeping the lights on**. They solve today's problem. They do not invest in self-service, automation, or developer experience. They do not treat the platform as a product that evolves, improves, and scales independently of headcount.

The result: your data strategy — no matter how well-designed — executes at the speed of ticket queues.

---

## The Default: Platform as a Product

The alternative is to treat the platform as a **product** whose customers are domain engineering teams. This shift is not semantic. It changes how you staff, how you prioritize, how you measure success.

**Product teams** ask: What do our customers (domain teams) need to do? How can we make that easy, safe, and fast? What is the interface? What is the developer experience? How do we measure adoption, satisfaction, time-to-value?

**Ops teams** ask: Is it running? Are the SLAs green? Did we close the ticket?

The platform-as-a-product model means:

- **Self-service interfaces** replace ticket queues. Domain teams provision Kafka topics via Terraform modules, not JIRA.
- **Golden paths** replace bespoke implementations. New pipelines clone a template repo with CI/CD, observability, and testing already wired.
- **Documentation and onboarding** replace tribal knowledge. A new engineer can ship their first pipeline in a day, not a month.
- **Product roadmaps** replace reactive backlogs. The platform team invests in features (e.g., "multi-cloud data replication") based on customer demand, not whoever shouted loudest in Slack.

### The Airport Analogy

Think of the data platform as an **airport**. Airlines (domain teams) serve passengers (business users). The airport provides runways, gates, fuel, baggage handling, air traffic control, security checkpoints. Airlines do not build their own runways. They do not staff their own TSA checkpoints. They focus on their core product: safe, efficient flights.

The airport operates as a **shared platform**. It invests in infrastructure that benefits all airlines. It enforces **guardrails** (safety regulations, gate scheduling protocols). It provides **golden paths** (standard boarding procedures, fuel truck protocols). Airlines can choose their own planes, routes, pricing, and customer experience — but they operate within the platform's interface.

The platform team is the airport. Domain teams are the airlines. Business users are the passengers.

If your platform team is custom-building a runway every time an airline (domain team) files a ticket, you're not running an airport. You're running a construction company that happens to enable flights as a side effect.

## What a Data Platform Team Actually Provides

So what does the platform team's **product** consist of? Six core capabilities:

### 1. Compute Runtime

**What it is:** The substrate for running workloads — batch jobs, streaming pipelines, ML training, API services.

**What it provides:** Kubernetes clusters (or managed equivalents like Cloud Run, ECS, Lambda), autoscaling, pod scheduling, resource quotas, multi-tenancy isolation.

**Golden path:** A Helm chart or Terraform module that deploys a Spark job with sane defaults (retry policies, resource limits, logging sidecars).

**Guardrail:** A policy-as-code rule (e.g., OPA, Kyverno) that prevents workloads from requesting unbounded CPU/memory or running as root.

### 2. Storage Substrate

**What it is:** Durable, scalable storage for data at rest — object stores (S3, GCS), databases (Postgres, BigQuery), caches (Redis).

**What it provides:** Multi-tiered storage (hot/warm/cold), encryption at rest, lifecycle policies, access controls, replication across regions.

**Golden path:** A Terraform module that provisions a versioned S3 bucket with encryption, lifecycle rules, and IAM bindings pre-configured.

**Guardrail:** A policy that prevents public-read buckets or unencrypted databases.

### 3. Connectivity

**What it is:** Networking, service discovery, ingress/egress, event backbones (Kafka, Pub/Sub, EventBridge).

**What it provides:** VPCs, service meshes (Istio, Linkerd), DNS, load balancing, API gateways, CDC connectors.

**Golden path:** A service mesh sidecar automatically injected into every pod, providing mTLS, retries, circuit-breaking, and distributed tracing with zero application code changes.

**Guardrail:** Network policies that enforce zero-trust (no pod-to-pod traffic unless explicitly allowed).

### 4. Security & Identity

**What it is:** Authentication, authorization, secrets management, encryption, audit logs.

**What it provides:** IAM roles, service accounts, Vault for secrets, KMS for encryption keys, RBAC for Kubernetes, SAML/OIDC for user auth.

**Golden path:** A CLI command (`platform create-service-account`) that provisions a K8s service account, binds it to a GCP Workload Identity, grants least-privilege IAM roles, and injects secrets into the pod via Vault.

**Guardrail:** A policy that prevents hardcoded secrets in code, enforces secret rotation every 90 days, and alerts on excessive IAM permissions.

### 5. Observability

**What it is:** Metrics, logs, traces, alerts, dashboards, SLOs.

**What it provides:** Prometheus + Grafana for metrics, Loki for logs, Tempo for traces, PagerDuty for alerting, pre-built dashboards for common services.

**Golden path:** Every service deployed via the platform's template automatically emits RED metrics (Rate, Errors, Duration) and logs are shipped to a centralized store with retention policies already configured.

**Guardrail:** A policy that requires every production service to define at least one SLO (e.g., 99.9% of requests under 500ms) and alerts fire when SLO budget is exhausted.

### 6. Self-Service Developer Experience

**What it is:** The interface domain teams interact with — CLI tools, web consoles, documentation, onboarding paths.

**What it provides:** A CLI (`platform`) that wraps Terraform, kubectl, and cloud SDKs. A docs site with quickstarts. Template repos for common patterns (streaming pipeline, batch job, API service). A Slack channel or support model for when things break.

**Golden path:** Run `platform init data-pipeline`, answer three prompts (data source, destination, transformation language), and get a repo with CI/CD, tests, observability, and deployment manifests pre-wired.

**Guardrail:** The `platform deploy` command runs policy checks (no unencrypted secrets, no public endpoints) before applying changes.

## Golden Paths vs Guardrails

**Golden paths** are the easy, pre-validated way to do common things. They are paved roads. You can still go off-road if you need to — but the golden path is faster, safer, and already approved by security/compliance/architecture.

**Guardrails** are constraints that prevent harmful patterns. They are the rumble strips and barriers that keep you from driving off a cliff. You cannot disable them without escalation.

Neither is optional. **Golden paths without guardrails** means domain teams can take shortcuts that create security holes, cost overruns, or operational disasters. **Guardrails without golden paths** means every domain team reinvents the wheel and fights policy engines every step of the way.

### Example: Paving a New Data Pipeline

A domain team wants to build a pipeline that reads from a source database, applies transformations, writes to the lakehouse, and triggers an ML model refresh. Here's the golden path:

1. **Clone the template:** `platform init data-pipeline --name customer-events`
2. **Configure source:** Edit `sources.yaml` to point at the source DB. The platform automatically provisions a CDC connector (Debezium) with change streams.
3. **Write transformation logic:** Add a Spark job in `transform/`. The template includes unit tests, CI checks, and a local development environment (Docker Compose with Postgres, Kafka, MinIO).
4. **Deploy:** Run `platform deploy --env staging`. The platform:
   - Provisions a Kafka topic with replication factor 3, retention 7 days
   - Deploys the Spark job to Kubernetes with resource limits, retry policies, and observability sidecars
   - Configures IAM bindings so the job can read from the topic and write to the lakehouse
   - Runs policy checks (no secrets in code, encryption enabled)
   - Outputs a Grafana dashboard URL pre-populated with RED metrics
5. **Monitor:** The domain team sees logs in Loki, traces in Tempo, metrics in Grafana. Alerts fire to their Slack channel if error rate exceeds 1%.

The domain team **did not**:
- Manually provision Kafka topics via JIRA ticket
- Write Kubernetes manifests from scratch
- Configure logging, tracing, or metrics instrumentation
- Request IAM roles from the security team
- Set up CI/CD pipelines

The platform team **did not**:
- Receive a ticket
- Manually provision infrastructure
- Debug a bespoke deployment

The guardrails **prevented**:
- Deploying with unencrypted secrets
- Running workloads without resource limits
- Writing to production before passing policy checks

## Platform Interface: The Contract

The **platform interface** is the contract between platform and domain teams. It defines:

- **APIs:** Terraform modules, Helm charts, CLI commands, REST APIs
- **Templates:** Starter repos for common patterns
- **SLAs:** Uptime guarantees, latency targets, support response times
- **Documentation:** Quickstarts, runbooks, architecture decision records
- **Support channels:** Slack, office hours, on-call escalation paths

A strong platform interface is **opinionated** but not rigid. It provides sane defaults for the 80% case and escape hatches for the 20%.

**Bad interface:** "Here's a Kubernetes cluster. Good luck."

**Good interface:** "Here are five templates (API service, streaming pipeline, batch job, ML training, RAG service). Clone one, customize it, deploy with `platform deploy`. If you need something custom, here's how to extend the base module. If you're stuck, post in #platform-help and we'll respond within 4 hours."

## Bridge from Module 1: Hybrid Ownership Model

In Module 1, you learned the **hybrid ownership model**: the platform team provides infrastructure and golden paths; domain teams own data products and pipelines within those guardrails.

Here's how that maps to platform foundations:

| **Platform Team Provides**        | **Domain Team Owns**                  |
|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| Kubernetes clusters               | Application code and pipelines        |
| Kafka topics (via self-service)   | Event schemas and consumer logic      |
| Lakehouse storage (S3, BigQuery)  | Data product schemas and SLAs         |
| Observability stack               | Service-specific dashboards and SLOs  |
| IAM roles and policies            | Role assignments for their services   |
| CI/CD templates                   | Pipeline definitions and test suites  |

The platform team does not dictate **what** domain teams build. It provides **how** they build it — the runtime, storage, networking, security, and observability substrate.

The domain team does not provision raw infrastructure. It uses the platform's APIs and templates to deploy services that meet business needs.

This separation of concerns unlocks **velocity**. Domain teams ship faster because they're not waiting on tickets. Platform teams scale because they're not provisioning resources one-off.

## Decision Framework: What to Centralize vs Delegate

Not everything belongs in the platform. Here's a framework:

**Centralize when:**
- It's undifferentiated heavy lifting (provisioning VPCs, configuring service meshes)
- It has compliance or security implications (IAM, encryption, audit logs)
- It benefits from economies of scale (shared observability stack, multi-tenant clusters)
- It's a common pattern across many domain teams (deploying a streaming pipeline, training an ML model)

**Delegate when:**
- It's domain-specific logic (transformation rules, business rules)
- It's a unique, one-off requirement (a bespoke integration with a legacy system)
- It requires deep domain knowledge (financial reporting rules, regulatory constraints)

**Example:** Kafka topic provisioning is **centralized** (common, benefits from standardization, has security implications). The event schemas and consumer logic are **delegated** (domain-specific).

## Failure Modes

Two common failure modes:

### 1. Over-Abstraction

The platform team builds a hyper-abstracted DSL or internal cloud that hides all complexity. Domain teams can only interact via a proprietary interface. When they need something custom, they're stuck.

**Symptom:** "Our internal platform doesn't support X, so we can't integrate with vendor Y."

**Fix:** Escape hatches. Let domain teams drop down to Terraform or raw Kubernetes when needed. The platform should make the easy things easy and the hard things possible.

### 2. Under-Investment

The platform team is understaffed or under-prioritized. Golden paths are outdated. Documentation is stale. Domain teams work around the platform instead of using it.

**Symptom:** "It's faster to just provision the resources manually than wait for the platform team's template to be updated."

**Fix:** Treat the platform as a first-class product. Staff it with product managers, engineers, and technical writers. Measure adoption, satisfaction, and time-to-value. Iterate.

## Summary

The data platform is not infrastructure that "just works." It's a **product** whose customers are domain engineering teams. The platform team provides compute, storage, connectivity, security, observability, and self-service developer experience — the six core capabilities that enable domain teams to ship data products without bottlenecks.

**Golden paths** are the paved roads: pre-validated templates and APIs that make common tasks easy. **Guardrails** are the constraints that prevent harmful patterns. Both are essential. Neither is optional.

The **platform interface** — APIs, templates, SLAs, documentation, support channels — is the contract between platform and domain teams. A strong interface is opinionated but not rigid, providing sane defaults for the 80% case and escape hatches for the 20%.

This bridges Module 1's **hybrid ownership model**: the platform team provides infrastructure and golden paths; domain teams own data products and pipelines within those guardrails. This separation unlocks velocity and scale.
